Getting Started

Welcome to LoanDr.!

LoanDr. is a powerful loan comparison tool designed for mortgage professionals. It helps you create side-by-side comparisons of different loan scenarios to find the best option for your clients.

How to Compare Loans

  1. Select a Loan Type: Choose from Conventional, FHA, VA, USDA, or ARM loans using the tabs at the top of each scenario.
  2. Enter Loan Details: Fill in the purchase price, down payment, interest rate, and loan term.
  3. Add Closing Costs: Enter lender fees, third-party fees, title fees, and any credits.
  4. Click Calculate: View the monthly payment breakdown, cash to close, APR, and total cost over the loan life.
  5. Add More Scenarios: Click the "+" button to add up to 6 different loan scenarios for comparison.
Pro Tip: Use the "Same Property" or "Different Properties" toggle at the top to indicate whether you're comparing different loan options for one property or loans for multiple properties.

Saving Your Work

  • Save: Click "Save" to store your scenarios to your account for later access.
  • Export: Download your scenarios as a file you can share or import on another device.
  • Auto-Save: Your work is automatically saved every 30 seconds to prevent data loss.

Generating Documents

Create professional comparison documents for your borrowers:

  1. Click "Generate Comparison Document for Borrower"
  2. Enter the borrower's name and property address
  3. Add your loan officer information
  4. Print, download, or email the document directly

Understanding Loan Types

C

Conventional Loans

Not backed by the government. Typically requires higher credit scores (620+) and down payments (3-20%). PMI required if down payment is less than 20%. Best for borrowers with strong credit and stable income.

F

FHA Loans

Insured by the Federal Housing Administration. Lower credit score requirements (580+ for 3.5% down). Requires upfront MIP (1.75%) and annual MIP. Great for first-time homebuyers or those with lower credit scores.

V

VA Loans

Guaranteed by the Department of Veterans Affairs. Available to eligible veterans, active-duty service members, and surviving spouses. No down payment required, no PMI, and competitive rates. Requires VA funding fee (can be waived for disabled veterans).

U

USDA Loans

Backed by the U.S. Department of Agriculture. For rural and suburban homebuyers with moderate income. No down payment required. Has upfront guarantee fee (1%) and annual fee (0.35%). Property must be in eligible rural area.

A

ARM (Adjustable Rate Mortgage)

Features a fixed initial rate that adjusts periodically. Common types: 5/1, 7/1, 10/1 (fixed years/adjustment frequency). Lower initial rates than fixed mortgages. Best for borrowers who plan to sell or refinance before the adjustment period.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is APR and why is it different from the interest rate? ▼

APR (Annual Percentage Rate) is the true yearly cost of borrowing, including interest AND fees like origination charges and points. The interest rate only reflects the cost of borrowing the principal. APR is always higher than the interest rate and is the best way to compare loans from different lenders.

What are discount points? ▼

Discount points are prepaid interest you pay at closing to lower your interest rate. One point equals 1% of the loan amount. For example, on a $300,000 loan, 1 point = $3,000. Generally, buying points is worth it if you plan to keep the loan for more than 5-7 years.

What is PMI and when is it required? ▼

PMI (Private Mortgage Insurance) protects the lender if you default on the loan. It's required on conventional loans when your down payment is less than 20%. PMI can be removed once you reach 20% equity. FHA loans have MIP (Mortgage Insurance Premium) which works similarly but often lasts the life of the loan.

How is cash to close calculated? ▼

Cash to close = Down Payment + Closing Costs + Prepaids - Credits. This is the total amount you need to bring to the closing table. Prepaids include items like property taxes, homeowner's insurance, and prepaid interest.

What's the difference between Same Property and Different Properties comparison? ▼

"Same Property" means you're comparing different loan options (like FHA vs Conventional) for the same house. "Different Properties" means you're comparing loans for different homes your client is considering. This helps contextualize the comparison for your borrowers.

How do ARM loans work? ▼

ARM loans have a fixed rate for an initial period (e.g., 5 years in a 5/1 ARM), then adjust periodically based on an index plus margin. Rate caps limit how much the rate can change at each adjustment (periodic cap) and over the life of the loan (lifetime cap). ARMs typically start with lower rates than fixed mortgages.

Can I save and share my comparisons? ▼

Yes! Click "Save" to store scenarios to your account. Use "Export" to download a .json file you can share with colleagues or import on another device. You can also generate a professional PDF document to share with borrowers.

Mortgage Glossary

Amortization

The process of paying off a loan through regular payments over time. Each payment includes both principal and interest.

Closing Costs

Fees paid at closing including origination fees, appraisal, title insurance, attorney fees, and other charges. Typically 2-5% of the loan amount.

DTI (Debt-to-Income Ratio)

Your monthly debt payments divided by your gross monthly income. Lenders typically want DTI below 43% for qualified mortgages.

Escrow

An account held by the lender to pay property taxes and insurance on your behalf. A portion of each mortgage payment goes into escrow.

LTV (Loan-to-Value Ratio)

The loan amount divided by the property value. An 80% LTV means you're borrowing 80% of the home's value (20% down payment).

PITI

Principal, Interest, Taxes, and Insurance - the four components of a typical monthly mortgage payment.

Prepaids

Items paid in advance at closing, including property taxes, homeowner's insurance, and per-diem interest.

Principal

The original loan amount borrowed, excluding interest. Each payment reduces your principal balance.

TRID (TILA-RESPA Integrated Disclosure)

Federal rules requiring standardized Loan Estimate and Closing Disclosure forms for most mortgages.

Underwriting

The process lenders use to evaluate your creditworthiness and determine if you qualify for a loan.
